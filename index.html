The Darkside of Chocolate













Basic Summary

Ghana and Côte d'Ivoire are the world's largest cocoa producers, making up approximately 60% of all cocoa harvested. But who exactly is harvesting the fruit of such a versatile product? Children! A survey conducted by Tulane University in 2013–14 showed that an estimated 1.4 million children aged 5–11 were subjected to work in cocoa-growing areas in Côte d'Ivoire. Those numbers, combined with the children in Ghana and other West African countries, rack up to a disgraceful minimum number of 2.3 million children (2018–2019, Statista)!


When discussing the topic of child labor in Ghana and Côte d'Ivoire, it would be rather ignorant to blame the parents of the children subjected to labor. Chocolate companies, such as Mars, come to these families with claims of providing their children with an education or money to support their education. But eventually, the companies fall through on their claims and pay the children and their families an inadequate salary. Many families face this harsh reality; to compensate, the children don't attend school and continue on the plantations with their family.






What is being done?

Chocolate companies like Nestlé will do anything to make a profit off of marginalized people in West Africa. Nestlé has been accused of trafficking six children from their home country of Mali (located north of Côte d'Ivoire) and putting them to work on a plantation. These children were subjected to beatings and other forms of abuse, with little to no compensation. All the while, Nestlé continued to bag billions of Swiss Francs yearly. Nestle Case




In November 2023, human rights activists filed a class action lawsuit against Mondelēz, Cargill, and Mars for intentionally enlongating the process of eliminating child labor from the cocoa used in their products for market gain. The plantiff in this case is a 10-year-old from Ghana who experienced the abuse of child labor starting at 4 years old (source) !

The Ivorian and Ghanaian governments are making moves to eliminate the maltreatment of these children and their families and, overall, put an end to this form of neocolonialism. The Ivorian government has passed multiple laws to protect children against gluttonous corporations. Ghana has done the same and is also attempting to bring more revenue from chocolate back to the country.
